        1. Executive Summary

        Project Atlas is positioned as a self-improving “Human Stories Intelligence Platform” rather than a video-generation tool. Its core asset is a proprietary graph connecting personalities, psychological arcs, audience segments, and story opportunities. Content generation is a downstream activity driven by this intelligence layer, not the starting point.

        The system is organized into six discovery/production layers plus an executive coordination layer, supported by an orchestration and data backbone (Temporal, PostgreSQL, Apache AGE, PGVector), an agent execution layer (KiloClaw), a content-generation layer (Gemma on Modal), and a strategy layer (DS-Star). External knowledge enters through a tiered set of third-party APIs.

        This document catalogs every agent, infrastructure component, data store, and external API referenced in the source discussion, organized for direct use as a build specification, and adds Monetization & Affiliate Strategy, Audience Growth & Distribution, and Human Merch Production sections to ensure the platform converts attention into diversified, durable, and compliant revenue and actually reaches an audience in the first place.

        2. Architecture Overview

        2.1 Six-Layer Agent Model

        Layer 1 — Trend Intelligence: detect what the world cares about right now.

        Layer 2 — Personality Intelligence: discover remarkable humans, known and hidden.

        Layer 3 — Story Intelligence: convert personalities into story universes and psychological arcs.

        Layer 4 — Audience Intelligence: determine who will watch and where.

        Layer 5 — Content Factory: produce scripts, hooks, voice, assets, thumbnails.

        Layer 6 — Commerce Intelligence: convert attention into merchandise and revenue.

        Above all six layers sits an Executive Layer (Chief Editor, Chief Intelligence/DS-Star, Knowledge Graph Curator) that decides what gets produced and when.

        2.2 Core Infrastructure Roles

              Component

              Role

              KiloClaw

              Managed agent execution environment: persistent 24/7 agents, scheduled/cron research, browser automation, multi-model routing, persistent memory cache. Performs research, reasoning, extraction, classification — does not own the system of record.

              Temporal

              Workflow orchestrator (“the real operating system”). Defines and retries multi-step workflows (e.g. nightly backlog generation) and calls KiloClaw / agent-service at each step.

              PostgreSQL

              System of record for all structured data — personalities, stories, scores, backlog.

              Apache AGE

              Graph extension on Postgres storing the Human Story Graph (personalities, relationships, archetypes).

              PGVector

              Vector store for embeddings of books, biographies, interviews, papers — enables agentic RAG.

              Gemma (on Modal GPUs)

              High-volume content production layer: scripts, repurposing, SEO, asset prompts. Fine-tunable on the platform's own successful output to build a proprietary style; 70–90% cheaper than premium models at scale.

              DS-Star

              Chief Data Scientist / strategy layer sitting above OpenClaw/KiloClaw. Performs pattern discovery, forecasting, opportunity scoring. Does not write scripts or generate content.

              Exa

              Semantic knowledge-discovery API; finds long-tail sources, hidden biographies, niche analysis that keyword search misses. Feeds PGVector/AGE.

              Brave Search API

              Real-time signal/trend discovery (“what's happening now”); complements Exa's depth-oriented discovery.

              agent-service (NestJS)

              Integration layer between Temporal and KiloClaw, exposing endpoints such as /agents/research, /agents/psychology, /agents/story-expansion, /agents/audience-analysis, /agents/hook-generation.

        2.3 Data Ownership Principle

        KiloClaw must never be the system of record. All durable state — personalities, story universes, opportunities, psychological arcs, audience segments, trend scores, backlog items — is stored in PostgreSQL / Apache AGE / PGVector. KiloClaw reads and writes but does not own.

        2.5 Copyright-Safe Content Principle

        Required pipeline: Sources → Fact Extraction → Knowledge Graph → Psychological Analysis → Original Narrative → Video. Prohibited pattern: Article → Rewrite → Video.

        Store only facts, events, dates, relationships, and reference metadata — never full articles, books, or biographies.

        Visual assets limited to public-domain archives, licensed assets, original AI-generated visuals, and self-created motion graphics.

        Avoid movie, TV, WWE, UFC, or broadcast footage unless properly licensed.

        DS-Star Specialized Scientist Roles

        DS-Star's strategy function is implemented as eight specialized scientist roles:

        Content Opportunity Scientist —

        Nightly analysis of Trends/YouTube/Reddit/Wikipedia/Search/News to recommend topics with scores and reasons.

        Audience Scientist —

        Analyze views, retention, comments, shares, geography, subscribers to discover who is actually watching.

        Story Universe Scientist —

        Compare retention across story types within one personality's universe (e.g. failure stories vs. product-launch stories) to direct future production.

        Archetype Scientist —

        Analyze engagement across thousands of videos to find which archetypes (underdog, rebel, visionary) drive retention vs. shares vs. watch time.

        Geographic Intelligence Scientist —

        Learn regional content preferences (e.g. India → Cricket Psychology, USA → Wrestling Psychology) to influence backlog ranking.

        Hidden Legends Discovery Scientist —

        Mine Wikipedia, books, biographies, and archives for high story-quality / low-coverage personalities.

        YouTube Scientist —

        Analyze CTR, watch time, retention, comments, likes, shares to find production-format insights (e.g. video length, best posting times by topic).

        Backlog Scientist —

        Rank all personalities and story opportunities into create-now / create-later / archive.

        Design rule: each scientist role above performs its actual analysis in Python/pandas (and, where the analysis is graph-shaped, via Apache AGE/networkx-style queries) running in the Railway agent-service at zero LLM cost — scoring, clustering, anomaly detection, and forecasting are arithmetic and statistical operations, not generation tasks. Gemini is invoked only for the narrow slice of the role's output that requires judgment a formula cannot produce: turning a ranked list into a plain-language reason, labeling a cluster with a human-readable description, or resolving a genuine tie/ambiguity. Most roles send Gemini a compact summary (tens to a few hundred tokens) rather than the underlying dataset, and several — Backlog Scientist's formula ranking, YouTube Scientist's anomaly detection on an unremarkable night — call Gemini on a minority of runs rather than every run, since there is nothing to interpret when nothing unusual occurred. Hidden Legends Discovery Scientist is the one role that remains LLM-dependent throughout, since judging the story-quality of a newly mined source is not reducible to a statistic; Python is still used there to pre-filter and novelty-score candidates before any of them reach a Gemini call.

        6.2 Cost Reduction Mechanisms

        Caching layer:

        search_cache table enforces a 30-day no-repeat-search rule per query, reducing search costs 70–90%.

        Event-based searching:

        only re-query when a real signal changes (trend shift, news event, Wikipedia/YouTube/Reddit spike) — not on a fixed schedule for every personality.

        Graph reuse:

        once a personality is discovered, expand new stories from the existing Apache AGE graph rather than re-searching externally.

        Workload separation:

        KiloClaw (research/reasoning, premium models) is kept separate from Gemma (high-volume content production, fine-tuned, cheap at scale).

        7. Content Organizing Principle

        The recommended channel strategy is a single master channel (“Human Psychology & Great Lives”) organized by archetype and theme rather than by country or by individual personality — mirroring how Netflix organizes by genre rather than by nationality.

        Global content universe, India-first distribution:

        ranking engine weights India highest initially, gradually increasing US/Europe/Australia influence as the audience grows.

        Archetype-first discovery:

        agents research archetypes (rebel, underdog, visionary, strategist) and map discovered people onto them, rather than researching people directly.

        Personality franchises:

        each personality is treated as a franchise (e.g. Steve Jobs → 100+ story opportunities) with dedicated story universes and episode sequencing, not a single biography video.

        Backlog composition target:

        30% famous personalities (attract viewers) / 40% moderately known / 30% hidden legends (differentiation moat).

        8. Monetization & Affiliate Strategy

        The architecture in Sections 1–7 forecasts revenue (Revenue Optimization Agent) but has no execution layer that actually captures it, no affiliate or sponsorship infrastructure, and no mechanism for realized revenue to influence the backlog. This section closes those gaps with a new Monetization Intelligence sub-layer, concrete data model additions, platform-specific playbooks, an affiliate program map, a sponsorship pipeline, an owned-audience strategy, a phased rollout, and a daily revenue floor mechanism.

        8.1 Strategic Principles

        Diversify beyond ad RPM.

        Ad revenue alone is volatile and platform-controlled. Affiliate income, sponsorships, and owned-audience products should each become a meaningful, independent revenue line within 12 months.

        Revenue is a scoring input, not just an output.

        Realized revenue per video/personality must feed back into the backlog ranking engine, not just engagement metrics.

        Own the audience, not just the channel.

        Email, Discord/Telegram, and membership lists must be built from day one so the business survives any single platform's algorithm or policy change.

        Match monetization to archetype.

        Different archetypes (Strategist, Builder, Survivor, Visionary) naturally pull different affiliate and sponsor categories — monetization should be planned per archetype, not bolted on generically.

        8.2 New Agents: Monetization Intelligence Sub-Layer

        Added inside Layer 6 (Commerce Intelligence), alongside the existing Merch Opportunity, Product Discovery, Regional Culture, and Revenue Optimization Agents.

              Agent

              Purpose

              Key Actions

              Ad Revenue Optimization Agent

              Maximize ad RPM within each video without harming retention.

              Recommends mid-roll count/placement by video length; flags advertiser-unfriendly content before publish; tracks YPP/Shorts Fund eligibility; monitors RPM by niche and geography.

              Affiliate Management Agent

              Turn every relevant story into a trackable affiliate placement.

              Selects the right affiliate program per story/archetype; generates and rotates tracked links; inserts them into descriptions, pinned comments, and on-screen cards; A/B tests placement and copy; reconciles click and conversion data weekly.

              Sponsorship Matching Agent

              Build a repeatable brand-deal pipeline instead of ad-hoc outreach.

              Matches archetype/audience profile to advertiser categories; maintains a CRM-style pipeline (prospect → outreach → negotiation → delivered → paid); tracks rate benchmarks by view tier.

              Owned Audience Agent

              Decouple revenue from platform algorithm risk.

              Manages CTA placement for email/Discord/Telegram capture; runs lead magnets (free guides, archetype quizzes); segments subscribers by archetype interest for targeted offers.

              Community Monetization Agent

              Convert the most engaged viewers into recurring revenue.

              Manages channel memberships, Patreon-equivalent tiers, and paid courses/digital products tied to high-performing personality franchises; tracks churn and LTV.

        8.4 YouTube Revenue Playbook

              Lever

              Action

              Ad RPM

              Qualify for YouTube Partner Program early; use 2–4 mid-rolls on videos over 8 minutes (Ad Revenue Optimization Agent decides count by retention curve, not a fixed rule); avoid demonetization triggers (violence/controversy framing) flagged by the Fact Verification Agent's content-safety pass.

              Shorts

              Shorts revenue share is lower per view than long-form ads but compounds at volume; treat Shorts primarily as a discovery/funnel layer into long-form and affiliate links, not a standalone revenue target.

              Channel Memberships / Super Thanks

              Enable once subscriber base is established; tie membership perks to personality franchises (e.g., early access to a new “Story Universe” episode).

              End screens / cards

              Use to route viewers to (a) the next video in the same Story Universe, (b) the email/Discord capture page, and (c) the top-performing affiliate link for that archetype.

        8.11 Daily Revenue Floor Mechanism

        Sections 8.1–8.10 establish revenue streams and a feedback loop, but none of them guarantee a minimum dollar amount on any given day. A daily floor requires treating revenue as a constraint the daily workflow must satisfy, not just a forecast it reports on.

        Separate the floor from the upside.

        Ad RPM and affiliate clicks are too variable to be the floor — they are the upside. The floor itself should come from fixed-revenue sources: monthly sponsorship retainers and channel memberships, both contracted or recurring rather than view-dependent. Size the retainer and membership base so their combined monthly total, divided by 30, already covers the minimum daily target before any ad or affiliate dollars arrive.

        Make publishing volume a hard SLA.

        The Temporal daily workflow should not be allowed to complete until a minimum publish count clears (e.g. 1 long-form video + 5 Shorts), enforced as a workflow gate rather than a target — because daily revenue is fundamentally volume × conversion, and a thin publishing day guarantees a floor miss regardless of how well any single video performs.

        Maintain an evergreen vault as a buffer.

        The Backlog Scientist tags a standing pool of personalities/archetypes with proven, repeatable revenue_actuals performance, not just engagement. When the Revenue Optimization Agent's same-day forecast trends below the floor, the Chief Editor Agent pulls from this vault and publishes a known earner instead of gambling on unproven trend content.

        Force a minimum monetization attach rate.

        The Affiliate Management Agent treats affiliate link insertion as a publish-blocking gate, not an optional step — every video carries at least one tracked, archetype-matched affiliate CTA, so floor revenue does not depend on a manual step being remembered.

        Track the floor in real time and define compensating actions.

        Roll revenue_actuals up daily against the floor target. When the Revenue Optimization Agent's intra-day forecast shows a miss coming, trigger a predefined response — boost an evergreen vault video, run a flash affiliate push, or pull a sponsorship deliverable forward — rather than logging the shortfall after the fact.

        9. Audience Growth & Distribution Strategy

        Sections 1–8 assume content reaches an audience. It does not, by default. YouTube and Instagram are algorithm-mediated platforms: distribution is earned through early engagement signals, not granted because a video is well-researched. This section defines how the platform earns views, converts viewers into subscribers, and converts subscribers into link clicks — closing the gap between "content is produced" and "content is actually seen, followed, and acted on."

        9.1 Platform Algorithm Mechanics

        Each platform decides distribution using different primary signals. Production and publishing decisions should be tuned per platform rather than treated as one undifferentiated "upload."

              Platform

              YouTube Shorts

        9.2 The Discovery-to-Subscribe Funnel

        Growth happens in a sequence; weakness at an earlier stage caps everything after it, so each stage needs its own design, not just "good content."

        1. Impression — the algorithm decides whether to show the thumbnail/title or feed card to a cold viewer at all.

        2. Click — the thumbnail, title, and (on Reels/Shorts) the first frame decide whether the viewer commits to watching.

        3. Retention — the first 3–5 seconds and overall pacing decide whether the viewer stays past the hook and through the middle.

        4. Action — a well-timed ask (subscribe, follow, click the link) converts an engaged viewer into a tracked asset instead of a one-time view.

        5. Return — series structure (Story Universes, recurring archetypes) gives a reason to come back without being re-discovered by the algorithm each time.

        Comment engagement:

        Community-sourced topics:

        surfacing audience questions/comments as future story angles (feeding back into the Coverage Gap Agent) both improves targeting and makes the audience feel ownership over what gets made next.

        9.10 Daily Learning & Feedback Loop

        Sections 9.8-9.9 define the agents and metrics needed for growth, but without a daily cadence and explicit attribution, performance data stays descriptive instead of actionable. This subsection defines the mechanism that turns yesterday's analytics into a concrete change in tomorrow's production.

        Pull platform analytics daily, not weekly.

        YouTube Analytics API and Instagram Insights API expose CTR, average view duration, retention by timestamp, traffic source, subscriber conversion, and link clicks within hours of publish. A daily pull (rather than a weekly or monthly review) is what makes same-week correction possible.

        Tag every video with its production variables at publish time.

        Hook type, thumbnail style, archetype, title pattern, video length, and posting time should be logged per video before results come in. Without this tagging, outcomes can be tracked but never attributed to a cause.

        Compare against a rolling per-archetype baseline, not an absolute bar.

        Different archetypes and formats have different normal retention and CTR ranges; each video should be scored against its own archetype/format's trailing 30-day average so wins and losses are measured correctly.

        Diagnose the specific failure or success mode, not just the score.

        Low CTR with strong retention indicates a thumbnail/title problem on an otherwise good video; strong CTR with an early drop-off indicates a hook that overpromised; good early retention with a mid-video cliff isolates a pacing problem in a specific section. The daily process should output this diagnosis per video.

        Change one production variable at a time where possible.

        If thumbnail style and hook structure both change on the same video, a result cannot be attributed to either. This materially degrades what the loop can learn, and should be treated as a production discipline rule, not a suggestion.

        Route the diagnosis into the agents that act on it the next day.

        The Thumbnail/Title CTR Agent updates which patterns it tests next using yesterday's CTR results; the Retention Agent flags script structures correlated with drop-off so the Narrative and Hook Agents avoid repeating them; the Backlog Scientist re-ranks archetypes/personalities using actual outcomes rather than predictions only.

        10. Human Merch Production & Envato Integration

        Sections 3.6 and 8 treat merchandise as something the Commerce Intelligence layer can detect and forecast, but they implicitly assume the resulting artwork can simply be generated and uploaded. In practice, leading print-on-demand platforms — Redbubble specifically — apply disclosure requirements, upload-volume caps, and elevated scrutiny to AI-generated submissions, and bear-the-risk IP exposure falls on the uploading account either way. This section adds a human-in-the-loop production layer: agents continue to do all research and opportunity detection, but execution — the actual creation of sellable artwork — is assigned to and completed by human designers, with humans uploading the finished work themselves. It also adds an Envato sourcing capability so designers can work from human-made raw material instead of a blank canvas, together with an explicit compliance gate addressing Envato's licensing terms.

        10.1 Strategic Rationale

        Redbubble does not categorically ban AI-generated content, but its Content and IP policies impose real friction and risk that make agent-only merch production an unreliable path to sellable listings.

        Mandatory AI disclosure.

        Redbubble requires sellers to declare AI-generated content via an explicit disclosure checkbox/dropdown at upload. Misrepresenting AI art as hand-drawn or undisclosed is a policy violation that risks listing removal or account action.

        Tiered daily upload caps for AI-tagged content.

        Disclosed AI content is subject to materially lower daily upload limits than human-made content — roughly 5/day for new accounts, 20/day for established sellers with 100+ sales, and 50/day for invite-only "Boosted Artist" accounts — which caps how fast an agent-only pipeline could ever scale on-platform even if fully compliant.

        IP-infringement risk sits with the uploader.

        AI image generators are statistically more likely to reproduce elements of copyrighted training data (characters, logos, styles) without the uploader realizing it, and Redbubble's IP enforcement holds the account, not the model provider, responsible — making unreviewed agent-generated artwork a higher-variance legal exposure than human-created original work.

        The practical conclusion: agents remain the research and opportunity-detection engine — they identify what should be made and why, tied to events, festivals, and trends already detected by the Trend Intelligence and Commerce Intelligence layers — but a human designer performs the actual creative execution, and a human account performs the actual upload, keeping the platform inside Redbubble's higher-volume, lower-risk human-content lane.

        These additions extend Layer 6 (Commerce Intelligence) with a human-task-assignment pipeline. They do not replace the existing Merch Opportunity, Product Discovery, Regional Culture, or Revenue Optimization Agents — they consume those agents' output and turn it into an assignable, trackable, human-completed task.

        10.5 Envato Licensing Compliance Gate

        This subsection documents a specific, user-confirmed finding from research into Envato Elements' license terms, recorded explicitly so it is on record as something to verify with Envato or legal counsel before relying on it at scale — it is not a confirmed green light.

        The restriction.

        The Envato Elements license prohibits reselling or redistributing items "as-is," prohibits "merchandising" (an end product where the Envato item itself is the primary value driver), and restricts use in "on-demand" services where the end customer customizes the product/artwork themselves.

        The exception the pipeline relies on.

        The same license explicitly permits end products where "skill and effort have been applied to incorporate the Item into a larger design," and permits use as product packaging. The intended pattern in this pipeline is squarely aimed at this exception: a human designer must meaningfully transform any Envato-sourced element into a larger original design, not simply place a downloaded asset onto a blank product and upload it.

        The unresolved ambiguity.

        Research surfaced genuine disagreement across official and community sources on whether a fixed-design print-on-demand platform like Redbubble — where the seller, not the end customer, fixes the final design before listing it for sale — falls inside Envato's "on-demand"/"merchandising" restriction or inside its "incorporated into a larger design" exception. Sources do not converge on a single answer, and Envato's own documentation does not name Redbubble-style fixed-design POD specifically.

        Required compliance gate.

        The Submission Review Agent must treat any designer_task with non-empty envato_asset_references as requiring an explicit incorporated_description before approval — a human-readable statement of how the Envato asset was transformed or incorporated, not just downloaded and placed. Tasks where Envato material was used as moodboard/reference only (and does not appear in the final file) should be tagged usage_role = reference and carry no compliance burden.

        Action required before scaling.

        Before relying on Envato Elements sourcing at meaningful volume, this licensing interpretation should be verified directly with Envato support/legal or outside counsel. Until that verification happens, Envato sourcing should be treated as a productivity aid for designers (mockups, texture/reference material, fonts) rather than a guaranteed-compliant supply chain, and the compliance gate above should default to caution — reject or request revision when incorporated_description is vague or merely describes placement rather than transformation.

        10.6 End-to-End Workflow

        1. Detect — Trend Discovery Agent, Merch Opportunity Agent, and an event/festival calendar feed identify a time-sensitive or evergreen merch opportunity (e.g. a Stoicism-themed product ahead of New Year's resolution season, or a cricket-archetype product ahead of a major tournament).

        2. Brief — the Merch Task Brief Agent converts the opportunity into a designer-ready brief: product type, theme, archetype, region, motif, and deadline tied to the event's relevance window.

        3. Source reference material — the extended Asset Planner Agent queries the Envato Elements MCP server for relevant stock photos, mockups, fonts, and graphic elements, attaching them to the brief as envato_asset_references with usage_role set to reference or mockup.

        4. Assign — the Designer Assignment Agent routes the brief to an available human designer matched on skill and current workload, prioritizing time-sensitive briefs nearing their relevance window.

        5. Create — the human Designer produces original artwork, using any sourced Envato material as reference, mockup, or a meaningfully transformed/incorporated component — not as a placed, unmodified asset — and submits the finished file.

        6. Review — the Submission Review Agent checks the submission against the brief, runs an originality/IP-similarity pass, and applies the Envato compliance gate from 10.5 to any task with non-empty envato_asset_references.

        7. Upload — on approval, the Upload Agent publishes the human-created artwork to Redbubble (and other configured POD platforms) under correct human-creator disclosure, logging the live listing URL back to designer_tasks.

        8. Feed back — listing performance (via Revenue Optimization Agent / revenue_actuals, Section 8.3) flows back into the Merch Opportunity Agent and Backlog Scientist so future briefs favor product types, themes, and designers that actually convert.

        This workflow keeps agents in the role they are strongest at — continuous research, opportunity detection, and brief generation at scale — while keeping a human in the loop for the two steps (creative execution and platform upload) where Redbubble's policies and IP-risk allocation make human authorship materially safer than full automation.

        11. Infrastructure Deployment & Inference Cost Governance

        This section documents how the agent fleet defined in Section 3 is distributed across hosting infrastructure, the data-security policy governing which model providers may be used for which workloads, and the concrete cost-control mechanisms applied to keep inference spend low given that most agent calls repeat the same instructions and context against a continuous stream of different items.

        11.1 Deployment Topology

        The agent fleet is split across three hosts based on workload type, not on a single uniform runtime:

        Railway — Data Science / DS-Star bucket.

        Hosts Temporal, PostgreSQL (with Apache AGE and PGVector as extensions on the same instance), and the agent-service running the Chief Intelligence Agent (DS-Star) and its eight Scientist roles (Content Opportunity, Audience, Story Universe, Archetype, Geographic Intelligence, Hidden Legends Discovery, YouTube, and Backlog Scientists). These agents are colocated with the graph and vector store specifically because their workload is dominated by repeated DB round-trips — pattern discovery, cross-agent correlation, nightly backlog re-scoring — and placing them elsewhere would add network latency and egress cost for every query with no offsetting benefit.

        Modal (GPU) — conditional, self-hosted bucket.

        Reserved for open-weight inference (Gemma) where self-hosting genuinely undercuts API pricing. At current channel volumes only the Script Agent is scoped here. Trend Discovery, Demand Forecast, and Coverage Gap are volume-triggered candidates for this bucket but should remain on the OpenClaw/Hostinger bucket until their call volume is high enough that GPU-seconds beat the now very low DeepSeek/Qwen flash-tier API pricing documented in 11.3 below — at present-day pricing that crossover point is materially further out than it was when only Gemini Flash-tier pricing was the comparison.

        Hostinger VPS — OpenClaw orchestration bucket.

        Runs the OpenClaw runtime for the remaining ~37 agents (intelligence, story, audience, content-factory, monetization, growth, and merch agents from Section 3), calling out to paid LLM APIs rather than hosting any model locally. A modest VPS (2–4 vCPU / 4–8GB RAM) is sufficient for pure API orchestration at 1–5 channel volume; sizing should be revisited upward only if any agent in this bucket performs live web browsing/scraping rather than calling an LLM on pre-fetched search results, since headless browser sessions carry a materially heavier memory footprint than text-only API calls.

        11.2 Model & Data Security Policy

        All production agent traffic must run on the paid tier of whichever provider it uses; no agent may route through a free API tier, since free tiers across every provider in this stack carry a data-training exposure risk that the paid tiers explicitly disclaim.

        Anthropic, OpenAI, Google (Gemini API).

        Paid-tier usage is not used for training by default, with provider-specific log retention terms (e.g., Anthropic's standard API log retention). These three providers are the default for any agent reasoning over proprietary story material, audience/personality graph data, or anything not already public.

        DeepSeek and Qwen (Alibaba).

        Both providers' paid tiers also claim no training on API inputs, but data residency and jurisdiction differ materially from the providers above. Given that difference, DeepSeek and Qwen should be restricted to agents working over non-sensitive, already-public data — trend scanning, demand/coverage classification, and similar high-volume candidate-scoring work — and should not be used for agents reasoning over proprietary story, personality-graph, or unreleased content data until that data-residency question is deliberately revisited, not defaulted into.

        This policy is a condition on model selection, not a recommendation: the cost-control mechanisms in 11.3 below assume every model swap stays inside this boundary.

        11.3 Inference Cost Control Mechanisms

        Prompt and context caching.

        Caching only matches an exact prefix, so every agent prompt must be structured with static content (system instructions, schema, persona) first and dynamic content (the specific item being processed) last — interleaving the two defeats caching entirely regardless of provider.

        DeepSeek.

        Caching is automatic and requires no setup: a cache hit on the input prefix costs $0.003/M versus $0.14/M on a miss, roughly 45x cheaper, with no separate storage fee. This is the simplest, lowest-maintenance saving in the stack and should be the default expectation for any DeepSeek-routed agent with a stable prompt template.

        Gemini.

        Cached reads cost roughly 10% of the standard input rate, but Gemini uniquely charges a separate cache storage fee ($1.00/M tokens/hour on Flash, $4.50/M tokens/hour on Pro). Caching only pays off if the cached content is reused often enough within the storage window to clear that fee — this should be checked per agent rather than enabled by default. DS-Star's nightly graph/vector snapshot is the clearest candidate, since all eight Scientist roles can share one cached read of the same snapshot instead of each role re-sending it.

        Cross-agent shared reference caching.

        The fastest way to clear Gemini's storage fee is to cache content that many different agents read, not just content one agent repeats to itself. Campaign briefs, brand guidelines, and the compliance documents already referenced in this specification — the AI-disclosure requirement in 10.1, the Envato-compliance gate in 10.5, the FTC affiliate-disclosure requirement in 10.8 — are read by Asset Planner, Thumbnail Agent, Script Agent, Submission Review Agent, and the Upload Agent's compliance gate alike. Caching that material once means every one of those agents' calls reads it at the 90% discount instead of each agent independently paying full input price for the same static document, which clears the storage fee far faster than caching content only one agent reuses.

        Setting the TTL.

        TTL should follow a break-even calculation, not the 1-hour default or an arbitrary round number: storage cost equals tokens times the hourly rate times TTL hours, and that cost only clears once enough calls have read the cache within the window to exceed it at the 90% discount. Since storage is billed for the full TTL regardless of how much of it is actually used, a campaign brief or compliance document that changes roughly daily should get a TTL sized to the fleet's actual call density across the day (a multi-hour window covering the bulk of that volume, refreshed when the source document changes) rather than an hourly refresh cycle that re-incurs the cache write more often than the document's real update frequency requires.

        Anthropic / OpenAI.

        Follow the same general pattern as Gemini — cheap cached reads, short default TTL, extendable — relevant if any Pro-tier agent is routed through these providers for the cross-model validation described below.

        Batch processing.

        Any workload that does not need an instant response should run through the provider's batch API for the standing 50% discount: DS-Star's nightly pass, and any non-real-time classification sweep (trend scanning, coverage-gap scoring) that already runs on a schedule rather than reacting to a live event. This applies cleanly to Gemini, Anthropic, and OpenAI's batch APIs; DeepSeek and Qwen's batch-discount availability should be confirmed directly before being counted on, since their base pricing is already low enough that batch savings matter less there than on the Gemini/Claude side.

        Multi-item batching within a single call.

        Distinct from the batch API above: where an agent's context window allows it, multiple candidate items should be processed in a single call rather than one call per item, so the static system-prompt overhead is amortized across all items in that call instead of being paid once per item. For the highest-volume agents (Trend Discovery, Demand Forecast, Coverage Gap), this is a larger lever than caching alone, since it reduces the number of calls outright rather than discounting the repeated portion of each one.

        Output token discipline.

        Output tokens are priced 2-4x input tokens across every provider in this stack. Every Flash-Lite/Turbo-tier agent should be constrained to structured, schema-only output (JSON, no narrated reasoning in the response) — verbose chain-of-thought-style output in production responses is the most expensive place for waste to hide, and it compounds at the call volumes these agents run at.

        Conditional cross-model validation.

        A second-opinion check from a different model provider is warranted only for the highest-stakes outputs where a same-model self-check is structurally weak — primarily the Fact Verification Agent's claims about real people or historical events, and the Cultural Context / Human Meaning Agents' judgment calls on sensitive cultural representation. This should run as conditional escalation (only when the primary model's confidence is low or the claim names a real person or culture) or as a random sampling audit (cross-validate roughly 5-10% of outputs), not as a blanket double-check on every call — applying it universally would double the Pro-tier cost line and erode the tiering savings documented above. The cross-check does not need to be the most expensive available model; a structurally different provider (different training lineage) catches a different class of error than a same-company escalation, so a mid-tier model from a separate provider is often sufficient.

        Non-generative task offloading (Jina AI).

        A number of agent sub-tasks are not generation tasks at all — they are retrieval, ranking, or classification tasks that are currently handled by spending an LLM call on them. Jina AI provides a suite of purpose-built, non-generative APIs (Reader, Embedding, Reranker, Classifier) sharing one pool of tokens, including a 10,000,000-token free allowance for new API keys and roughly $0.05/M tokens thereafter — meaningfully cheaper than routing the same task through even the lowest LLM tier, since these calls have no expensive output-generation cost.

        Reader API.

        Converts any URL into clean, LLM-ready text, handling JS rendering and scraping itself. This removes the need for headless-browser infrastructure on the Hostinger VPS for any agent doing live web research — Trend Discovery, Demand Forecast, Coverage Gap, and Hidden Legends Agent call Reader to fetch and clean source material, then feed the distilled text into the cheap Qwen-Turbo/DeepSeek Flash call rather than running a Chromium session directly on the OpenClaw host.

        Embedding API.

        Generates the vectors written into and queried from PGVector, as a cheap alternative to an LLM provider's embedding endpoint — used by Personality Graph Agent, Story Atomizer Agent, Audience Mapping Agent, Knowledge Graph Curator Agent, and DS-Star's retrieval step on the Railway bucket.

        Reranker API.

        Given a query and a batch of candidates, returns them ranked by relevance for a fraction of the cost of asking an LLM to do the same ranking via prompting. Applies to Coverage Gap Agent, Demand Forecast Agent, and Personality Discovery Agent's shortlisting step, and DS-Star's Backlog Scientist prioritization — narrowing the candidate set cheaply before it reaches a Pro-tier reasoning call, reinforcing the funnel discipline the tiering strategy already depends on.

        Classifier API.

        Zero/few-shot classification for the yes/no and category decisions currently handled by an LLM call — Submission Review Agent's originality/policy check, the Upload Agent's AI-disclosure and FTC-disclosure compliance gate, and Trend Discovery's categorization tagging.

        Jina is restricted to the same non-sensitive-data boundary as DeepSeek and Qwen under 11.2 unless its data-handling terms are separately confirmed: safe for public web content and candidate ranking/classification, not yet assumed safe for proprietary story or personality-graph text without that confirmation.

        Computation-first delegation (Python before LLM).

        The largest single reduction available is not a cheaper model — it is not calling a model at all for work that is actually arithmetic. Several agents, especially DS-Star's eight scientist roles (Section 3.7), were originally scoped as LLM calls over raw retrieved data, but their underlying task is statistical: ROAS calculation, anomaly detection, clustering/segmentation, and forecasting are pure Python/pandas operations with no generative component. The corrected design runs that computation in-process in the Railway agent-service at zero LLM cost, and sends Gemini only a compact summary of the result — typically tens to a few hundred tokens — for the one part of the job a formula cannot do: turning numbers into a plain-language interpretation, labeling a cluster, or resolving a genuine ambiguity. Several roles (Backlog Scientist's weighted-formula ranking, YouTube Scientist's anomaly check on an unremarkable night) call Gemini on a minority of runs rather than every run, since there is nothing to interpret when the computation surfaces nothing unusual.

        Applied to DS-Star, this took the bucket's nightly LLM spend from an estimated $30-35/month (driven by feeding large retrieved-context dumps into Pro-tier calls for all eight roles every night) down to roughly $1-3/month, with the Chief Intelligence Agent's synthesis call shrinking proportionally since it now aggregates eight short summaries instead of eight raw data dumps. The same principle should be applied wherever else an agent's job is fundamentally computational rather than generative — Revenue Optimization Agent and Ad Revenue Optimization Agent's spend-forecasting work (Sections 3.6/8.2) and Engagement Prediction Agent's anomaly-detection work are the clearest additional candidates: compute in Python, escalate to Gemini only on the flagged exception or the closing interpretation line, not on every call.

        General rule: an agent earns an LLM call when its job is narrative judgment, ambiguity resolution, or cross-agent synthesis — not when its job is arithmetic, clustering, or a deterministic scoring formula. Every agent in Section 3 should be checked against this rule before being assigned a model tier in the table above, since a cheaper model assigned to a task that should not be calling an LLM at all is still spending money that computation-first delegation would remove entirely.

        Prompt compression (LLMLingua-2).

        LLMLingua-2 is a small token-classification model, not a generative LLM, so it runs cheaply on the Hostinger VPS CPU as a pre-processing step before a request reaches any paid API. It compresses the input side of a prompt 4-20x depending on task, at a measured cost of roughly 1.5 benchmark points of quality at the high end of that range. The strategic point is that it only reduces input-token cost, not output-token cost, so where it gets applied should be decided by each agent's input-to-output ratio, not applied uniformly across the fleet.

        Where it pays off most.

        Input-heavy agents — those that ingest a large block of retrieved or extracted text relative to what they generate — see the closest to the full 75% reduction on their input line. This is Trend Discovery, Demand Forecast, and Coverage Gap Agent (large source/search-result text in, a short score and label out); Hidden Legends Discovery Scientist and any agent consuming Jina Reader output (the extracted web text is the input; the candidate verdict is the output); Knowledge Graph Curator Agent and the DS-Star scientist roles before their context is condensed into a short summary per 11.3's computation-first lever; and Fact Verification Agent when checking a claim against a long reference passage.

        Where to skip or limit it.

        Output-heavy generation agents — Hook, Vibe, Narrative, Script — have a small input and a large generated output, so compressing the input barely moves their total cost; applying it there adds a quality risk for little saving. Pro-tier reasoning agents (Psychological Arc, Human Meaning, Archetype, Chief Editor, Story Opportunity Intelligence) should either skip compression entirely or use the conservative end of the ratio (4-10x, not the maximum 20x), since the nuance in their input context is exactly what the premium model is being paid to reason over — losing 1.5 points of fidelity matters more on a judgment call than on a high-volume classification pass.

        Sequencing with caching.

        Compression must not run live on the static system-instructions portion of a prompt, since caching depends on an exact-match prefix and per-call compression output is not guaranteed to be byte-identical between runs. The static prefix should be compressed once, offline, and that fixed compressed version cached as the new prefix; live, per-request compression is then applied only to the dynamic per-item content that was never going to be cached anyway. Implemented in this order, the two levers stack — the cached prefix is already small, and the uncached dynamic portion is compressed before it is sent — rather than one undermining the other.

        Net placement: this lever runs last in the pipeline, after computation-first delegation has removed whatever doesn't need an LLM call at all and after Jina has offloaded retrieval/ranking/classification — it compresses whatever input is left over once those two levers have already done their work, which is also why its benefit is concentrated on the input-heavy research and intelligence agents rather than the generation-heavy content-factory agents.

        These mechanisms are cumulative, not alternatives: computation-first delegation removes the LLM call entirely wherever the task is arithmetic rather than generative, tier-matching determines which model an agent uses by default for what remains, prompt compression shrinks the input side of whatever is left before it is sent, caching and multi-item batching reduce what each call costs once that model is chosen, batch-API scheduling discounts anything that can tolerate latency, conditional cross-model validation adds a bounded, targeted cost only where the risk of a single model's blind spot is high enough to justify it, and non-generative offloading to Jina removes whole categories of call from the LLM bill entirely where the task was never really generation in the first place.

        11.4 Per-Host Cost Levers

        The mechanisms in 11.3 target repeated LLM-call cost specifically. The levers below sit outside that scope — they apply at the architecture, infrastructure, and lifecycle level, and are organized by how soon each is actionable rather than by host.

        Actionable now.

        Cross-channel deduplication of shared intelligence work.

        Trend Discovery, Demand Forecast, and Coverage Gap currently run implicitly per channel, but much of what they detect — global trending topics, search-demand shifts — is shared across channels and personalities rather than channel-specific. Running that analysis once and fanning the result out to every channel that needs it, instead of re-running it per channel, is the same fixed-cost-amortization logic that made per-video cost higher at 1 channel than at 5 in the worked examples earlier in this document — it should be designed in now, before a per-channel calling pattern gets built into the orchestration layer and becomes harder to unwind.

        Structured/schema-enforced outputs.

        When an agent's output fails to parse (malformed JSON, a missing required field), the orchestrator typically retries the call and pays for the input tokens a second time. Enforcing function-calling or strict JSON-schema output modes, supported natively by Gemini, Anthropic, OpenAI, DeepSeek, and Qwen, eliminates most of that retry cost. Small per call, but compounds at the volume the Flash-Lite/Turbo tier runs at.

        Storage and CDN cost discipline for generated media.

        Every lever above this point addresses LLM inference cost; Asset Planner, Voice Director, and Thumbnail Agent also produce video, audio, and image files that must be stored and served. Object storage with no egress fee (e.g. Cloudflare R2, Backblaze B2) versus a provider that charges per-GB egress (e.g. S3) can matter meaningfully once file sizes and view counts scale, and should be priced out explicitly rather than defaulted to whatever Railway or Hostinger bundles in.

        Actionable once volume or history exists.

        Distilling Pro-tier judgment into a small fine-tuned model.

        For a specific, narrow, recurring decision (e.g. FTC-disclosure flagging, a single archetype-scoring judgment), a small open-weight model can be fine-tuned on the Pro-tier model's own historical outputs as training labels once enough production history exists. Once validated, this replaces an ongoing per-call API cost with a one-time training cost plus near-zero self-hosted inference. This requires months of labeled history to train against, so it is a later-stage lever, not a day-one one.

        Self-hosted embeddings at scale.

        The same crossover logic that governs the Modal/Gemma decision in 11.1 applies to embeddings: Jina's embedding API is cheap at low volume, but if embedding volume grows large enough across Personality Graph, Story Atomizer, and DS-Star's retrieval step, self-hosting an open embedding model on Modal could eventually undercut Jina's per-token rate. Not worth doing at current volume; worth re-checking once embedding call volume is actually known.

        Committed-use or enterprise pricing.

        Not available at 1-5 channel scale, but once monthly spend with a single provider (most likely Gemini, given the Pro-tier concentration) becomes predictable, most providers offer committed-use or enterprise discounts in the 10-30% range. Revisit once a few months of cost_actuals data exist rather than planning around this now.

        These levers do not compound with each other the way the mechanisms in 11.3 do — most are independent architectural or lifecycle decisions rather than a stacked pipeline, so each should be evaluated on its own merits against the cost_actuals data once it exists, not assumed to deliver a fixed percentage on top of the savings already documented above.
